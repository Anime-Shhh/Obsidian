1. _**Step 1: Find the derivative $\vec{r}'(t)$** First, we take the derivative of each component of $\vec{r}(t) = \langle 3 \cos(2t), 3 \sin(2t), 3t \rangle$ with respect to $t$ (using the chain rule for the trigonometric functions):
    
    - $x'(t) = \frac{d}{dt}[3 \cos(2t)] = 3(-\sin(2t))(2) = -6 \sin(2t)$
    - $y'(t) = \frac{d}{dt}[3 \sin(2t)] = 3(\cos(2t))(2) = 6 \cos(2t)$
    - $z'(t) = \frac{d}{dt}[3t] = 3$
    
    So, the velocity vector is: $$ \vec{r}'(t) = \langle -6 \sin(2t), 6 \cos(2t), 3 \rangle $$ **Step 2: Find the magnitude $|\vec{r}'(t)|$** Next, we calculate the magnitude (the speed) of this derivative vector: $$ |\vec{r}'(t)| = \sqrt{(-6 \sin(2t))^2 + (6 \cos(2t))^2 + (3)^2} $$ $$ |\vec{r}'(t)| = \sqrt{36 \sin^2(2t) + 36 \cos^2(2t) + 9} $$ Factor out the $36$ from the sine and cosine terms: $$ |\vec{r}'(t)| = \sqrt{36(\sin^2(2t) + \cos^2(2t)) + 9} $$ Using the fundamental Pythagorean trigonometric identity, $\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1$, this simplifies beautifully: $$ |\vec{r}'(t)| = \sqrt{36(1) + 9} $$ $$ |\vec{r}'(t)| = \sqrt{45} $$ $$ |\vec{r}'(t)| = \sqrt{9 \cdot 5} = 3\sqrt{5} $$ **Step 3: Evaluate the arc length integral** Now we set up the integral for the arc length over the interval $-8 \le t \le 4$: $$ L = \int_{-8}^{4} 3\sqrt{5} , dt $$ Since $3\sqrt{5}$ is just a constant, the integration is very straightforward: $$ L = \left[ 3\sqrt{5} t \right]_{-8}^{4} $$ $$ L = 3\sqrt{5} (4) - 3\sqrt{5} (-8) $$ $$ L = 3\sqrt{5} (4 + 8) $$ $$ L = 3\sqrt{5} (12) $$ $$ L = 36\sqrt{5} $$ **Step 4: Compute the final decimal answer** Finally, we calculate the exact numerical value of $36\sqrt{5}$: (1/2)
    
2. $$ 36 \times \sqrt{5} \approx 36 \times 2.236067977... \approx 80.498447... $$ The problem asks for the answer to two decimal places, which rounds to **80.50**. (2/2)
### Relevent info:
1. This specific type of problem—finding the arc length of a helix or curve—is a classic Calculus 3 staple. To solve it comfortably, here are the 4 prerequisites you need, and exactly where they show up:**
    
    1. The Chain Rule for Derivatives (Calculus 1)
    
    **You need to be comfortable taking derivatives of trigonometric functions where the inside isn't just $t$.
    
    - **Where it was used:** In Step 1, when we found the derivative of $3 \cos(2t)$.
    - **The rule:** $\frac{d}{dt} [\cos(kt)] = -k \sin(kt)$. You have to remember to pull that $2$ out from the inside of the $2t$, which turns the $3$ into a $6$.
    
    **
    
    2. 3D Vector Magnitude (Calculus 3 / Linear Algebra)
    
    **You need to know how to find the length (magnitude) of a 3D vector. It's essentially just the Pythagorean theorem extended into 3 dimensions.
    
    - **Where it was used:** In Step 2. If a vector is $\vec{v} = \langle x, y, z \rangle$, its magnitude is $|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}$.
    - **The trap:** Remember to square the negative signs away! $(-6 \sin(2t))^2$ becomes a positive $36 \sin^2(2t)$.
    
    **
    
    3. The Pythagorean Trig Identity (Pre-Calculus)
    
    **This is the most important "trick" in Calculus 3. You need to know that **$\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1$**.
    
    - **Where it was used:** In Step 2, under the square root. (1/2)
    
2. - **Why it matters:** Calc 3 professors _love_ designing problems like this. Whenever you take the magnitude of a vector that has a sine and cosine component, they usually set it up so that you can factor out the constant (like the $36$) and the $\sin^2(2t) + \cos^2(2t)$ perfectly collapses into $1$. This turns a horrifying integral into a simple constant. If your square root isn't simplifying cleanly in an arc length problem, check your algebra—it's usually designed to cancel out!
    
    **
    
    4. Basic Definite Integrals (Calculus 1)
    
    **You need to know how to evaluate an integral over bounds $[a, b]$.
    
    - **Where it was used:** In Step 3. Because the trick in Step 2 wiped out all the $t$'s, you were just left integrating a constant: $\int c , dt = ct$. Then you plug in the top bound minus the bottom bound.
    
    **Summary** If you ever get stuck on one of these, 90% of the time the mistake happens either by forgetting the Chain Rule (forgetting to multiply by 2) or messing up the algebra right before applying the $\sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1$ identity. (2/2)

## Q2 ![[image-5 3.png]]
1. **Step 1: Find the first and second derivatives** Given $\vec{r}(t) = \langle \cos(t), \sin(t), 5t \rangle$, we take the derivative twice:
    
    - Velocity vector: $\vec{r}'(t) = \langle -\sin(t), \cos(t), 5 \rangle$
    - Acceleration vector: $\vec{r}''(t) = \langle -\cos(t), -\sin(t), 0 \rangle$
    
    **Step 2: Evaluate the vectors at the specific point ($t = 0$)** _Crucial shortcut:_ Do not try to do the algebraic cross product with all the sines and cosines first. Plug in $t=0$ immediately so you're just doing basic arithmetic with numbers!
    
    - $\vec{r}'(0) = \langle -\sin(0), \cos(0), 5 \rangle = \langle 0, 1, 5 \rangle$
    - $\vec{r}''(0) = \langle -\cos(0), -\sin(0), 0 \rangle = \langle -1, 0, 0 \rangle$
    
    **Step 3: Calculate the Cross Product $\vec{r}'(0) \times \vec{r}''(0)$** Set up the determinant with our numerical vectors: $$ \begin{vmatrix} \vec{i} & \vec{j} & \vec{k} \ 0 & 1 & 5 \ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{vmatrix} $$
    
    - $\vec{i}$ component: $(1)(0) - (5)(0) = 0$
    - $\vec{j}$ component: $-[(0)(0) - (5)(-1)] = -(0 - (-5)) = -5$
    - $\vec{k}$ component: $(0)(0) - (1)(-1) = 0 - (-1) = 1$
    
    So, the cross product vector is $\langle 0, -5, 1 \rangle$. (1/2)
    
2. **Step 4: Find the required magnitudes** Now we just need the lengths of two vectors to plug into our curvature formula.
    
    1. **Numerator:** Magnitude of the cross product $\langle 0, -5, 1 \rangle$$$ |\vec{r}'(0) \times \vec{r}''(0)| = \sqrt{0^2 + (-5)^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{0 + 25 + 1} = \sqrt{26} $$
    
    2. **Denominator base:** Magnitude of the velocity vector $\langle 0, 1, 5 \rangle$$$ |\vec{r}'(0)| = \sqrt{0^2 + 1^2 + 5^2} = \sqrt{0 + 1 + 25} = \sqrt{26} $$
    
    **Step 5: Compute final Curvature ($\kappa$)** Plug the numbers back into the formula from the beginning: $$ \kappa(0) = \frac{|\vec{r}'(0) \times \vec{r}''(0)|}{|\vec{r}'(0)|^3} $$ $$ \kappa(0) = \frac{\sqrt{26}}{(\sqrt{26})^3} $$ This simplifies nicely. Using exponent rules, $\frac{x}{x^3} = \frac{1}{x^2}$: $$ \kappa(0) = \frac{1}{(\sqrt{26})^2} = \frac{1}{26} $$ **Step 6: Convert to decimal** The problem asks for two decimal places. $1 \div 26 \approx 0.03846...$ Rounded to two decimal places, the answer to type in the box is: **0.04** (2/2)

    ### The "No Calculus" Explanation
    
    Let's look at the pieces of the equation: $\vec{r}(t) = \langle 3 + 2\cos(t), 2, -1 - 2\sin(t) \rangle$
    
    1. **The $y$ piece is just the number $2$.** Because there is no $t$ attached to it, the curve never goes up or down on the y-axis. It is completely flat, drawn on a piece of paper sitting at $y=2$.
    2. **The $x$ and $z$ pieces have $\cos(t)$ and $\sin(t)$ multiplied by the same number ($2$).** In math, whenever sine and cosine are paired up like this with the same number in front of them, they draw a perfect circle. That number in front ($2$) is the **radius** of the circle.
    
    **The Golden Rule of Circles:** A perfect circle bends exactly the same amount everywhere. Its curvature never changes, no matter what $t$ is. The curvature formula for _any_ circle is always just: **$1 \div \text{radius}$** Since our radius is $2$, our curvature is $\frac{1}{2}$. That's it. No matter what ugly fraction they put for $t$, the answer is $\frac{1}{2}$. ---
    
    ### The Calculus Proof (Step-by-Step)

    - Derivative of $3 + 2\cos(t) = -2\sin(t)$
    - Derivative of $2 = 0$
    - Derivative of $-1 - 2\sin(t) = -2\cos(t)$
    
    Velocity $\vec{r}'(t) = \langle -2\sin(t), 0, -2\cos(t) \rangle$ **Step 2: Find Speed (Magnitude of Velocity)** Just like the very first problem we did together: $$ |\vec{r}'(t)| = \sqrt{(-2\sin(t))^2 + 0^2 + (-2\cos(t))^2} $$ (1/2)

    - Derivative of $-2\sin(t) = -2\cos(t)$
    - Derivative of $0 = 0$
    - Derivative of $-2\cos(t) = 2\sin(t)$ _(negative cosine turns into positive sine)_
