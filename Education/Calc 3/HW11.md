### The Core Concept (The Physics)
1. 
    Imagine you are driving a car.
    
    - **Position ($\vec{r}$):** This is the GPS coordinate of exactly _where_ your car is right now.
    - **Velocity ($\vec{v}$):** This is your speedometer. It tells you _how fast_ you are moving and in what direction.
    - **Acceleration ($\vec{a}$):** This is your gas pedal or your brakes. It tells you how much your velocity is _changing_.
    
    In math, to go from Position $\rightarrow$ Velocity $\rightarrow$ Acceleration, you take a "step down" using something called a **derivative**.
    
    ---
    
    ### Step 1: Find Velocity $\vec{v}(t)$
    
    We start with our Position equation: $\vec{r}(t) = \langle 4t, -5t^5, 3t^3 + 4 \rangle$ Let's apply our rule to each piece to find the Velocity equation:
    
    - First piece ($4t$): The $t$ disappears. It becomes **$4$**.
    - Second piece ($-5t^5$): $5 \times -5 = -25$, exponent goes down to $4$. It becomes **$-25t^4$**.
    - Third piece ($3t^3 + 4$): $3 \times 3 = 9$, exponent goes down to $2$. The $+4$ disappears. It becomes **$9t^2$**.(1/2)
    
2. So, our velocity equation is: $\vec{v}(t) = \langle 4, -25t^4, 9t^2 \rangle$
    
    ### Step 2: Plug in $t = -1$ for Velocity
    
    The problem asks for $\vec{v}(-1)$, which just means "plug $-1$ in for $t$". _Watch out for negatives raised to exponents! A negative times a negative is a positive._
    
    - First piece: Just $4$. There is no $t$ to plug into, so it stays **$4$**.
    - Second piece: $-25(-1)^4$. Because the exponent $4$ is even, $(-1 \times -1 \times -1 \times -1)$ becomes a positive $1$. So, $-25 \times 1 =$ **$-25$**.
    - Third piece: $9(-1)^2$. Because the exponent $2$ is even, $(-1 \times -1)$ becomes a positive $1$. So, $9 \times 1 =$ **$9$**.
    
    **Velocity Box Answers:** 
    $$\vec{v}(-1) = [ $ 4 $ ] \quad [ $ -25 $ ] \quad [ $ 9 $ ]$$ ---
    
    ### Step 3: Find Acceleration $\vec{a}(t)$
    
    To find acceleration, we take our Velocity equation and apply the exact same rule again to take another "step down". Velocity equation: $\vec{v}(t) = \langle 4, -25t^4, 9t^2 \rangle$
    
    - First piece ($4$): It's a plain number with no $t$. It disappears and becomes **$0$**.
    - Second piece ($-25t^4$): Multiply $4 \times -25 = -100$. Exponent goes down to $3$. It becomes **$-100t^3$**.
    - Third piece ($9t^2$): Multiply $2 \times 9 = 18$. Exponent goes down to $1$ (which is just a plain $t$). It becomes **$18t$**.
    
    So, our acceleration equation is: $\vec{a}(t) = \langle 0, -100t^3, 18t \rangle$
    
    ### Step 4: Plug in $t = -1$ for Acceleration
    
    Now we plug $-1$ into our acceleration equation. _Watch the negatives again! This time the exponents are odd._
    
    - First piece: Just **$0$**.
    - Second piece: $-100(-1)^3$. Because $3$ is an odd number, $(-1 \times -1 \times -1)$ stays $-1$. So, $-100 \times -1 =$ positive **$100$**.
    - Third piece: $18(-1)$. A positive times a negative is a negative. It becomes **$-18$**.
    
    **Acceleration Box Answers:** $$\vec{a}(-1) = [ $ **0** $ ] \quad [ $ **100** $ ] \quad [ $ **-18** $ ]$$

1. **Step 1: Find the velocity vector $\vec{v}(t)$ by integrating acceleration $\vec{a}(t)$** We integrate each component of the acceleration vector: $$ \vec{v}(t) = \int \vec{a}(t) , dt = \int \langle 3t, 6\sin(t), \cos(5t) \rangle , dt $$ $$ \vec{v}(t) = \left\langle \frac{3}{2}t^2 + C_1, -6\cos(t) + C_2, \frac{1}{5}\sin(5t) + C_3 \right\rangle $$ **Step 2: Use the initial velocity $\vec{v}(0)$ to find the constants** We are given $\vec{v}(0) = \langle -4, -2, 4 \rangle$. By plugging in $t=0$, we can solve for our $C$ constants:
    
    - **$x$-component:** $\frac{3}{2}(0)^2 + C_1 = -4 \implies C_1 = -4$
    - **$y$-component:** $-6\cos(0) + C_2 = -2 \implies -6(1) + C_2 = -2 \implies C_2 = 4$
    - **$z$-component:** $\frac{1}{5}\sin(0) + C_3 = 4 \implies 0 + C_3 = 4 \implies C_3 = 4$
    
    So, the specific velocity vector is: $$ \vec{v}(t) = \left\langle \frac{3}{2}t^2 - 4, -6\cos(t) + 4, \frac{1}{5}\sin(5t) + 4 \right\rangle $$ **Step 3: Find the position vector $\vec{r}(t)$ by integrating velocity $\vec{v}(t)$** We integrate each component of the velocity vector to get position: $$ \vec{r}(t) = \int \vec{v}(t) , dt = \int \left\langle \frac{3}{2}t^2 - 4, -6\cos(t) + 4, \frac{1}{5}\sin(5t) + 4 \right\rangle , dt $$
    
    - **$x$-component:** $\int \left(\frac{3}{2}t^2 - 4\right) , dt = \frac{3}{2}\left(\frac{t^3}{3}\right) - 4t = \frac{1}{2}t^3 - 4t + K_1$
    - **$y$-component:** $\int (-6\cos(t) + 4) , dt = -6\sin(t) + 4t + K_2$
    - **$z$-component:** $\int \left(\frac{1}{5}\sin(5t) + 4\right) , dt = \frac{1}{5}\left(-\frac{1}{5}\cos(5t)\right) + 4t = -\frac{1}{25}\cos(5t) + 4t + K_3$
    
    $$ \vec{r}(t) = \left\langle \frac{1}{2}t^3 - 4t + K_1, -6\sin(t) + 4t + K_2, -\frac{1}{25}\cos(5t) + 4t + K_3 \right\rangle $$ **Step 4: Use the initial position $\vec{r}(0)$ to find the final constants** (1/2)
    
2. We are given $\vec{r}(0) = \langle 4, 5, 5 \rangle$. Plugging in $t=0$ again:
    
    - **$x$-component:** $\frac{1}{2}(0)^3 - 4(0) + K_1 = 4 \implies K_1 = 4$
    - **$y$-component:** $-6\sin(0) + 4(0) + K_2 = 5 \implies 0 + 0 + K_2 = 5 \implies K_2 = 5$
    - **$z$-component:** $-\frac{1}{25}\cos(0) + 4(0) + K_3 = 5 \implies -\frac{1}{25}(1) + K_3 = 5 \implies K_3 = 5 + \frac{1}{25} = \frac{126}{25}$
    
    ### Final Answer
    
    Here is what you will enter into the three boxes from top to bottom: **Top box ($x$-component):** $$ \frac{1}{2}t^3 - 4t + 4 $$ **Middle box ($y$-component):** $$ -6\sin(t) + 4t + 5 $$ **Bottom box ($z$-component):** $$ -\frac{1}{25}\cos(5t) + 4t + \frac{126}{25} $$

**Step 1: Find the Horizontal Velocity at $t = 2$** Horizontal velocity is the derivative of the $x$-position with respect to time, $v_x(t) = \frac{dx}{dt}$.

1. Take the derivative of $x(t)$:$$v_x(t) = \frac{d}{dt}(3t^2 + 4t) = 6t + 4$$
2. Plug in $t = 2$:$$v_x(2) = 6(2) + 4 = 12 + 4 = 16$$

**Step 2: Find the Vertical Velocity at $t = 2$** Vertical velocity is the derivative of the $y$-position with respect to time, $v_y(t) = \frac{dy}{dt}$.

1. Take the derivative of $y(t)$:$$v_y(t) = \frac{d}{dt}(3t^2 + 3) = 6t$$
2. Plug in $t = 2$:$$v_y(2) = 6(2) = 12$$

**Step 3: Find the Speed at $t = 2$** Speed is the magnitude of the velocity vector. The formula is $\text{Speed} = \sqrt{(v_x)^2 + (v_y)^2}$.

1. Use the values we found at $t = 2$:$$\text{Speed} = \sqrt{(16)^2 + (12)^2}$$
2. Calculate the squares:$$\text{Speed} = \sqrt{256 + 144}$$
3. Add and take the square root:$$\text{Speed} = \sqrt{400} = 20$$

### Final Answers

Here is what you will enter into the three boxes:

- **Horizontal Velocity =** `16`
- **Vertical Velocity =** `12`
- **Speed =** `20`

    - $x(0) = \sqrt{3}\sec(0) = \sqrt{3}(1) = \sqrt{3}$
    - $y(0) = \sqrt{3}\tan(0) = \sqrt{3}(0) = 0$
    - $\vec{r}(0) = \sqrt{3}\vec{i} + 0\vec{j}$ (or just $\sqrt{3}\vec{i}$)
    
    --- **Step 2: Part b - Find the velocity vector $\vec{v}(t)$** Take the derivative of $\vec{r}(t)$ with respect to $t$.
    
    - **$i$-component:** $\frac{d}{dt}[\sqrt{3}\sec(t)] = \sqrt{3}\sec(t)\tan(t)$
    - **$j$-component:** $\frac{d}{dt}[\sqrt{3}\tan(t)] = \sqrt{3}\sec^2(t)$
    - $\vec{v}(t) = (\sqrt{3}\sec(t)\tan(t))\vec{i} + (\sqrt{3}\sec^2(t))\vec{j}$
    
    --- **Step 3: Part c - Find the acceleration vector $\vec{a}(t)$** Take the derivative of $\vec{v}(t)$ with respect to $t$. This requires the product rule and chain rule.
    
    - **$i$-component:** $\frac{d}{dt}[\sqrt{3}\sec(t)\tan(t)]$
        
        - Using the product rule: $f'g + fg'$ where $f = \sec(t)$ and $g = \tan(t)$
        - $= \sqrt{3} [\frac{d}{dt}(\sec t)\cdot \tan(t) + \sec(t) \cdot \frac{d}{dt}(\tan t)]$
        - $= \sqrt{3} [(\sec t \tan t)\tan(t) + \sec(t)(\sec^2 t)]$
        - $= \sqrt{3}(\sec(t)\tan^2(t) + \sec^3(t))$
        
    - **$j$-component:** $\frac{d}{dt}[\sqrt{3}\sec^2(t)]$
        
        - Using the chain rule: $2 \cdot \sqrt{3}\sec(t) \cdot \frac{d}{dt}(\sec t)$
        - $= 2\sqrt{3}\sec(t)(\sec t \tan t)$
        - $= 2\sqrt{3}\sec^2(t)\tan(t)$
        
    - $\vec{a}(t) = \sqrt{3}(\sec^3(t) + \sec(t)\tan^2(t))\vec{i} + (2\sqrt{3}\sec^2(t)\tan(t))\vec{j}$
    
    --- **Step 4: Part d - Find the angle $\theta$ at $t = \pi$** First, evaluate the velocity and acceleration vectors at $t = \pi$. Recall that $\sec(\pi) = -1$ and $\tan(\pi) = 0$.
    
    1. **Velocity at $t = \pi$:** (1/2)
    
2. - $v_x(\pi) = \sqrt{3}(-1)(0) = 0$
        
        $v_y(\pi) = \sqrt{3}(-1)^2 = \sqrt{3}$
        $\vec{v}(\pi) = 0\vec{i} + \sqrt{3}\vec{j}$
        
    
    2. **Acceleration at $t = \pi$:**
        
        - $a_x(\pi) = \sqrt{3}((-1)^3 + (-1)(0)^2) = \sqrt{3}(-1) = -\sqrt{3}$
        - $a_y(\pi) = 2\sqrt{3}(-1)^2(0) = 0$
        - $\vec{a}(\pi) = -\sqrt{3}\vec{i} + 0\vec{j}$
